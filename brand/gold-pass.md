## 1. Brand Synthesis

**The core message.** One person — usually the most capable one — has quietly become the household's operating system: its memory, scheduler, preference archive, risk manager, and source of truth. The book's verdict is the brand's verdict: *"If the system collapses when you rest, you do not have a system. You have a person. And that person deserves infrastructure."* The work is making the invisible work visible enough to share — so the system carries the repetition and the humans carry the creation and meaning.

**The public positioning.** Christa Burger builds operating systems for real life. Boardroom to kitchen, the discipline is the same one she practices as a cybersecurity governance executive — and the book's single greatest bridge line makes the whole brand cohere: *"Governance is not bureaucracy. Governance is how love stops relying on telepathy."* She is not an AI consultant, a productivity guru, or a home-organization influencer. She is the person who noticed that households, teams, and lives fail the same way — invisible cognition outgrowing one person's nervous system — and who builds the mercy-shaped infrastructure that fixes it.

**The emotional promise.** *Mercy* — that's the book's most-used abstract noun, and it should be the site's undertone. The visitor who belongs here has been the disappearing act: competent, load-bearing, tired in a way that has no receipt ("The work is constant, but it disappears when it succeeds"). The promise is being seen, exonerated, and equipped: you are not disorganized, you are not failing, and you are allowed to stop being the only place the household can find itself.

**The practical promise.** Named, teachable machinery: care loops, defaults that beat discipline, a source of truth, done states, Hard-Week Mode, the weekly review, one-job AI helpers with job descriptions, judgment gates. And a starting bar deliberately on the floor: *"You are allowed to begin with dinner."* One loop. One default. One helper. A slightly better Tuesday.

**The strongest audience.** Whoever is the household's default operating system — named directly as the one who "knows the dentist's name, the dog's medication schedule, which kid needs new shoes." Women and caregivers explicitly, but the door is wide ("mother, father, partner, leader, caregiver, or human with a planner"). Second ring: leaders and professionals who sense their work-self and home-self are running the same broken pattern. NOT for: productivity maximalists, perfection seekers, anyone who wants to run the family like A Quarterly Business Review.

**The clearest "what I do."** *I make invisible work visible enough to share — at home with AI and care loops, at work with governance and decision clarity — so the system carries the repetition and the humans carry the meaning.*

**Signature themes and language to carry sitewide.** The Deciding · invisible cognition · care loops · "help is not the same as ownership" · defaults beat discipline · the source of truth ("if truth lives everywhere, truth lives nowhere") · Hard-Week Mode · dignity systems · Background Beasts · the Articulate Junk Drawer · one job per helper · "AI prepares. Humans decide." · judgment gates · peace before panic · "mercy with a recurring appointment" · "Not a perfect home. Not a robotic home. A more human one." · "Start small, then let it grow" · "Ordinary is where the relief enters."

**The surprising brand insight.** The book disciplines its own premise. Its anti-automation guardrails are more prominent than its automation: "A prayer does not need a checklist." "It can plan dinner. It cannot bless the meal." The Beauty Test asks whether a system makes the home more *alive*. This is the moat. Every AI-productivity site on the internet promises more output; this brand promises that the machine will be made to sit down — "long enough to remember it is not God." Lead with the humanity, let the AI be staff. Also structural: the book holds the Stable System until Chapter 23 on purpose ("Please do not open the book by asking an exhausted person to imagine a household forest full of agents"). The site should obey the same law: ponies are the glint, never the gate.

## 2. The Website Story

**The arc across three surfaces:** the homepage *names the wound and the builder*; the About page *proves the person*; the welcome email *begins the relationship with a gift*.

- **Homepage — the front porch.** A first-time visitor (from LinkedIn, a podcast, the book's back matter) should feel recognized within five seconds — not by a job title but by 5:30 p.m. It opens with the human problem, reveals that a governance executive is the one solving it (credibility as twist, not throat-clearing), presents the book as the front door, the vault as the free handshake, and the deeper work (governance, speaking, leadership) as rooms off the porch.
- **About — the proof.** One coherent story: the woman who governs enterprise risk by day realized she was ungoverned infrastructure at home, sat down with one uncomfortable question, and built her way out — kitchen first, philosophy second, ponies eventually. Faith named honestly, without a pulpit. Ends with who belongs here.
- **Welcome email — the first mercy.** Deliver the vault instantly, sound like her at the kitchen table, make one promise (one good letter, first word on the book), and close with the division of labor that runs the whole brand.
- **The main CTA everywhere:** *Get the free chapter + open the vault* (one email, both gifts) until launch day; then it flips to *Get the book* with the vault as secondary.
- **What the visitor should feel → understand → do:** Seen ("she knows about The Deciding") → oriented ("this is governance for real life, from someone who does it professionally") → moved to one small act (email in, chapter out) — the site practicing the book's own doctrine: start small, let it grow.

## 4. About Page Copy

> *Page hero eyebrow:* ABOUT
> *Title:* Christa Burger
> *Lead (italic):* Governance executive. Household operating system, retired. Builder of systems that let humans be more human.

**MY STORY**

## I was the system. Then I built one.

For twenty years I've worked in cybersecurity governance — the discipline of deciding how organizations handle data, risk, identities, and decisions when the stakes are real. I'm a Vice President of Cybersecurity. I know what good infrastructure looks like, and I know what happens to organizations that run on heroics instead of systems.

Which is why it took me embarrassingly long to notice that my own household was running on exactly that. Me — the memory, the scheduler, the preference archive, the risk manager, the place where the current answer lived. The realization didn't arrive as lightning. It arrived as one more stupid thing at 5:30 p.m., in a kitchen with a personality, holding the day's thousandth decision. I was not just doing tasks. I was maintaining context. And context is where the real work hides.

So I did what governance people do: I sat down with the uncomfortable question underneath the busy ones — *who are you, and what is your life for?* It took a week and felt like an existential audit. What surfaced was that I'm motivated by aliveness — building, writing, wandering the American West looking for petroglyphs, restoring old watches, my people. And a precise list of what made me feel nothing at all: the deciding, the remembering, the reconciling. Those became candidates for real infrastructure.

**WHAT I BUILT**

I started with dinner. One care loop. Then defaults, a source of truth, Hard-Week Mode, a weekly review — and eventually a team of small AI helpers, each with one job and a job description, because one giant assistant is just an articulate junk drawer. Yes, they're named after 1980s My Little Ponies. Yes, that turned out to be engineering: if metaphor makes a system easier to trust, remember, and use, metaphor isn't fluff. It's interface.

The result is not a perfect home and was never supposed to be. It's a home that knows how to recover. The systems plan meals, track the pantry, mind the thresholds, and brief my week — and what they actually returned was margin: presence, patience, creative capacity, the ability to rest without anything collapsing. The machine carries the repetition. The humans carry the meaning. I wrote *The Automated Household* because I kept meeting brilliant, exhausted people carrying entire households in their heads and calling it a personal failing. It is not a personal failing. It is a missing system.

**WHAT I BELIEVE**

My faith shapes all of it. I'm a Christian, and I believe technology is at its best when it serves human flourishing — and at its worst when it forgets its place. That's why my systems keep a Sabbath: the improving machine has to sit down sometimes, long enough to remember it is not God. I hold the same line professionally. AI prepares; humans decide. It can plan dinner; it cannot bless the meal. It can remember the vet appointment; it cannot love the dog.

The through-line of everything — the boardroom, the book, the kitchen, the stable of small helpers — is one conviction: governance is not bureaucracy. Governance is clarity. It's how love stops relying on telepathy, at every scale I've ever tested.

**WHO BELONGS HERE**

If you're the one your household or your team quietly runs on — the noticer, the rememberer, the single point of failure — you're who I write for. If you want a perfectly optimized life, I'd be wasting your time; ordinary is where the relief enters, and I'll keep saying so. Start with [the free chapter](/book), wander [the Automated Household](/automated-household), or [bring me the enterprise version](/work-with-me) of the same problem.

## 6. Strategic Notes

**Why these choices.** The old hero ("Build systems people can trust") was true but faced the boardroom; the book faces the person. "You were never meant to be the system" keeps the second person, names the wound in six words, and lets the governance credential arrive as a twist — the proof bar and Living Proof section do the résumé work so the headline doesn't have to. Every section now opens with the visitor's life, not your title.

**What I preserved from the book.** Its actual spine, in its own words: The Deciding, invisible cognition, care loops, the thesis sentence, "AI prepares. Humans decide," the telepathy line as the brand's unifying bridge, "Start small, let it grow" as the sitewide closing move, and — deliberately — the book's own pacing law: the Stable System stays a late-arriving delight, never the front door.

**What I elevated.** *Mercy* as the emotional undertone (it's the book's most-used abstract noun — the copy stays warm-blooded rather than efficient). The anti-automation guardrails ("sit down… not God," "bless the meal") — they're your moat against every AI-productivity site on earth. And the work-home bridge, which the old site implied but never said out loud in one sentence.

**What I left out, on purpose.** Pony names and Fern Gully from the homepage (Chapter 23's rule); "eliminate mental load" claims (the book says *transfer*, and the difference is the brand's honesty); productivity vocabulary entirely; the word "balance"; and heavy AI-tool specifics — the site sells judgment, not tooling.

**Flags for you.**
1. **Subtitle mismatch:** the manuscript's title page reads "A Field Guide to Transferring Mental Load, Building Household Systems, and Using AI to Make You More Human" — the site currently uses "Stop Being the System." I've used the manuscript subtitle for the book itself and kept "you were never meant to be the system" as campaign language. Confirm which subtitle ships to KDP and I'll align everything.
2. **Fern Gully** appears on the site but never in the manuscript (which uses forest imagery and "Caleb, the Forest Keeper"). Fine as a personal flourish — just decide whether the book's public world should adopt it or the site should soften it.
3. **Add-a-story slots:** the About page would take one more concrete household detail beautifully (the eight mustards are ready-made), and the Speaking page should eventually quote the book's case-file device (Apollo 13, Cinderella, hospital triage) as talk texture.
4. The homepage hero video still fits — lavender at night reads as calm-after-the-noise. Keep it.
