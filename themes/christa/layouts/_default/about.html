My Story

I build governance systems for complex work.

My background is cybersecurity governance, risk, compliance, and trust — the unglamorous but essential work of helping organizations decide how data, systems, identities, vendors, controls, customers, and leadership decisions should be handled when the stakes are real. As a Vice President of Cybersecurity, I live that work daily: risk made visible, customer trust made credible, and compliance made useful.

AI has made that work more urgent, but it did not invent the underlying problem. The deeper question is: what kind of decision system are we building, who does it serve, who is accountable, what human judgment must never be outsourced — and how do leaders stay whole while carrying the weight?

That is the thread through everything I do: enterprise governance, AI governance, public education, practical household automation, agent design, writing, and speaking. I run a household of seven — a husband, five children across multiple schools and a college campus, a housekeeper, and an ever-rotating ecosystem of animals. The systems I talk about are not theoretical productivity exercises. They are the smallest visible laboratory for the same principles: clarify ownership, reduce invisible labor, protect attention, make care repeatable, and leave the human more present than before.

What drives this work

My faith shapes all of it. I'm a Christian, and I believe technology is at its best when it serves human flourishing rather than consuming it. I'm deeply interested in the practical side of AI — but equally drawn to the questions underneath: what we automate, what we protect, who decides, and what remains human in an age optimized for speed.

This work sits somewhere between operational strategy, systems design, leadership formation, and pattern recognition. When I'm not building governance frameworks or teaching workshops, I'm usually wandering the American West looking for petroglyph panels, restoring vintage watches, or studying the strange ways civilizations leave traces of themselves behind.

Because in the end, systems tell stories. And the ones we build inevitably shape the people we become.
