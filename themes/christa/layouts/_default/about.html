My Story

Systems, risk, and the question of what it means to remain human

I've spent most of my career standing at the intersection of systems, risk, and human behavior. As a Senior Director in Cybersecurity Governance, Risk, and Compliance, I help organizations navigate the realities of deploying AI responsibly — in environments where the stakes are real and the buzzwords tend to outnumber the answers.

That work gave me an unusual vantage point into what AI actually looks like once it leaves the demo stage and collides with real people, real workflows, and real operational complexity. And somewhere along the way, that perspective spilled over into building, experimenting, and quietly automating parts of my own life.

I run a household of seven — a husband, five children across multiple schools and a college campus, a housekeeper, and an ever-rotating ecosystem of animals. The systems I talk about are not theoretical productivity exercises. They are actively running my kitchen, calendar, grocery planning, and household rhythms right now. What started as curiosity turned into practical systems that gave me something increasingly rare: margin.

What drives this work

My faith shapes all of it. I'm a Christian, and I believe technology is at its best when it serves human flourishing rather than replacing it. I'm deeply interested in the practical side of AI — but equally fascinated by the spiritual and cultural questions emerging alongside it: what we automate, what we value, what we forget, and what it means to remain human in an age increasingly optimized for speed.

This work sits somewhere between operational strategy, modern homesteading, systems design, and pattern recognition. When I'm not building automations or teaching workshops, I'm usually wandering the American West looking for petroglyph panels, restoring vintage watches, or studying the strange ways civilizations leave traces of themselves behind.

Because in the end, systems tell stories. And the ones we build inevitably shape the people we become.
